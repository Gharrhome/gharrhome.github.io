The Stakeholder Map — Who Is Speaking, Who Is Silent, And What They Actually Feel
🧑 The Informed Ordinary Person
The Johns and Julias — when they finally see the numbers
Awakening When people are shown the actual ratio — $334 spent on weapons per human, and less than $1 going to the WFP — the reaction isn't political. It's personal. It feels like betrayal. Like being lied to about what is even possible.
↗ Pattern synthesis from peace movement forums, anti-militarism blogs, global justice communities · 2024–2025
Rage The dominant discovered emotion is moral rage — not at an enemy, but at the framing. People are furious that the AI arms race is discussed as something new and alarming, while the $2.7 trillion baseline is treated as normal and invisible.
↗ ReThink Media survey (Nov–Dec 2024, n=1,789) · INET analysis · Global peace blogs
Numbness Before Awakening Most ordinary people do not understand this is the normal baseline. The number $2.7 trillion has no emotional anchor. It is too large to feel. The moment it becomes real — often via a per-person comparison ($334 per human) — the emotional response changes completely.
↗ Framing research · Political psychology · Survey patterns on abstract vs concrete numbers
Grief A secondary wave that follows the rage is grief — specifically for what has been quietly accepted. People feel grief for the children in Sudan, DRC, Haiti receiving half rations not because the money doesn't exist, but because it is actively going elsewhere.
↗ WFP reporting · UNODA signal · Humanitarian community blogs
🌍 The Global South Voice
Those who live the consequence, not just debate the numbers
Bitter Recognition For communities in Sudan, Afghanistan, South Sudan, and Haiti — where WFP rations have already been cut to 50–70% — the gap is not abstract. It is the mother skipping meals. The child receiving half the required nutrition. The pipeline break that arrives before the food does. For these communities, this is not a statistic. It is their daily architecture.
↗ WFP country reports 2025 · Field testimonies · UNOCHA humanitarian updates
Systemic Resignation Across the Global South, there is a deep and practiced resignation. Not the naïve kind — the earned kind. The kind that comes from watching development aid fall for the first time in six years while military budgets hit record highs in the same governments writing the cheques.
↗ ODA 2024 data · OECD DAC report · IPB global network · Peace advocacy communities
Naming The Architecture Activists and civil society in lower-income regions have been naming this architecture clearly for years: the guns-before-butter trade is not a bug in the system. It is the system. The AI arms race is not a new threat. It is the same logic accelerating.
↗ IPB 100+ endorsing organizations from 30 countries · Peace bureau statements 2024
🏘️ The Western Domestic Public
The poll-answering majority — and what they actually prioritize
Manufactured Consent Polling data reveals a striking contradiction: majorities consistently prioritize healthcare, education, and infrastructure over military spending. But when the same people are asked about defense "threats," fear overrides preference. The system requires enemies to justify itself. When there is no palpable monster, the money feels inexplicable.
↗ Chicago Council Survey 2024 (n=2,106) · Reagan Defense Survey Dec 2024 · INET analysis
The Framing Effect When asked about "the Pentagon budget," more people support cuts than when asked about "the defense budget." The same dollars. Different words. Different emotional response. This means a large latent public awareness exists — it just needs the right language to become visible action.
↗ ReThink Media / Network to Dismantle MIC survey (Nov–Dec 2024, n=1,789)
The Incomprehension Problem Most ordinary Western people have no calibrated sense of what $2.7 trillion means. When shown the comparison — 750 times the UN's entire regular budget, 277 times what WFP received — they often experience something close to cognitive dissonance. It does not fit any mental model of how the world works.
↗ Public perception research · INETECONOMICS commentary · Peace bureau communications
War Profiteering Consensus Across partisan lines, large majorities of Americans view war profiteering as a serious problem. Even many who support higher defense spending believe the system is corrupt and self-serving. This is one of the quietest areas of cross-partisan agreement in the entire political landscape.
↗ ReThink Media survey 2024 · INET "Trillion Dollar War Machine" analysis 2025
🏛️ The Official Signal
What governments and UN bodies are actually saying — and what they aren't doing
The UN Is Screaming Into A Void The UN Secretary-General released a landmark report in September 2025 called "The Security We Need." The language is extraordinary: the world is spending "750 times its regular budget on weapons." The trajectory is "unsustainable." The SDGs are "critically off-track." Almost no government has changed course.
↗ UN SG Report "The Security We Need" September 2025 · UNDP press release · SIPRI data
The Explicit Trade The UK cut official development assistance from 0.5% to 0.3% of GNI in 2025 and explicitly reallocated the difference to military spending. This is the guns-vs-butter trade made public, named, and executed without apparent electoral consequence. That silence from electorates is its own signal.
↗ IPI Global Observatory October 2025 · OECD DAC 2024 data
The NATO Ratchet NATO has now set a new target of 3.5% of GDP on defense. If met, this alone would add $474 billion per year to global military spending — equal to five times the entire annual WFP budget needed — and trigger additional reactive increases from China, Russia, and non-NATO powers.
↗ IPI Global Observatory · SIPRI 2024 · NATO communiqué analysis

Seduction Theory Diagnostic — What The Full Stakeholder Picture Reveals
The world is not ignoring the hunger-military trade-off.
It has never truly seen it. And the system is designed to keep it that way.

The Core Seduction Theory Finding: The dominant strategy in the global military-industrial complex is not suppression of truth — it is abstraction. Numbers so large they cannot be felt. Threats framed as existential. Development spending framed as charity. Military spending framed as necessity. When the numbers are made concrete and comparative — $334 per human on weapons vs. approximately $1.28 per human on the WFP — ordinary people do not feel indifference. They feel rage, grief, and betrayal. The emotion is there. The framing has been missing.

The AI arms race is not a new chapter. It is the same chapter with a new cover. The question Gharr is asking — "do ordinary people understand this is the normal baseline?" — is itself the most important act of communication possible right now. Because the answer is: almost universally, no. And when they find out, they are not okay with it.

The Win-Win Map: The signal from the jungle is not that people want disarmament at any cost. It is that they want honesty about the trade. When polled directly, people consistently prioritise healthcare, education, and infrastructure above defense. When asked about "national security," fear overrides this preference. The gap between those two states is where the real political energy lives — and it is almost entirely unoccupied by any current political movement.

The Stakeholder Asymmetry: The people most affected by the hunger-military trade — children in famine zones, displaced populations, communities receiving half-rations — have zero voice in the discourse that determines the ratio. The people with the most voice — defence contractors, NATO planners, technology executives — have the most to gain from keeping the baseline invisible. This is the map's most important structural feature.

What people feel when they understand: In the research and signal sampled from peace networks, advocacy communities, and ordinary people who have encountered the comparative framing — the sequence is consistent. First disbelief ("that can't be right"). Then verification. Then a specific kind of moral nausea. Then rage at the normalisation. Then grief for what has already been lost. Then, in a smaller number, the beginning of committed action. The map does not go from ignorance to indifference. It goes from ignorance to the full emotional range — and the system depends on the first state persisting.

The AI Arms Race as Accelerant: The current AI infrastructure spending by private tech companies — $650B+ in 2026 alone, heading toward $3–4 trillion by decade's end by some estimates — is beginning to fuse with military spending in ways that erase the boundary. The Pentagon declaring itself an "AI-first warfighting force," requiring all commands to appoint AI Integration Leads, makes this fusion explicit. The public conversation has not caught up. The baseline keeps growing before anyone has named it.

The Seduction Theory Verdict: The global community does not feel good about this. It just does not yet know the full shape of what it does not feel good about. The map exists to close that gap. The signal is real. The emotion is real. The political will is latent and available. The only missing element is the framing that makes the invisible visible.

"The global community's voice is always real. It is not always right. The distance between what people believe and what is actually happening is not a flaw in the map — it is the map's most important feature. Use it as a compass, not a verdict."
